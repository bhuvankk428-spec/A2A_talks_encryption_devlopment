
The research foundation for Quantum-Secure A2A begins with Google's 2017 SIGCOMM paper on 
QUIC, which introduced a UDP-based transport protocol that encrypts every packet 
end-to-end, uses Connection IDs so sessions survive IP changes, and eliminates 
head-of-line blocking through multiplexed streams — cutting Google Search 
latency by 8% and YouTube buffering by 18% at internet scale, making it the 
ideal transport for concurrent AI agent communication. Building on that, 
IETF RFC 8446 standardised TLS 1.3, stripping out every weak cipher from the previous
 two decades and designing the key exchange layer to be modular and extensible 
 — meaning entirely new cryptographic algorithms can be plugged in without
  changing anything else in the protocol. RFC 9001 then specified exactly
   how QUIC and TLS 1.3 work together: QUIC borrows TLS's handshake to 
   derive keys and then encrypts every UDP packet itself across four 
   independent encryption levels, which means swapping the key exchange algorithm
    in that handshake automatically secures every single byte of every QUIC 
    connection — a one-layer change with system-wide effect. That replacement
     algorithm is ML-KEM, standardised by NIST as FIPS 203 in August 2024 after 
     a 7-year competition: it is built on the Module Learning With Errors mathematical 
     hardness problem, which has no efficient solution for quantum or classical 
     computers, produces keys compact enough to fit inside a single QUIC packet,
      and is already deployed by default in Chrome, AWS, Cloudflare, and Signal.
       The original 2017 IACR academic paper behind ML-KEM explains why it can 
       be trusted — it proves through formal security reductions that breaking Kyber
        is mathematically equivalent to solving MLWE, a problem for which no efficient 
        quantum algorithm exists, with the Fujisaki-Okamoto transform providing full IND-CCA2 
        security against adaptive chosen-ciphertext attacks. A 2024 arXiv paper then 
        performed a precise quantum threat assessment of QUIC itself and found that only one component
         is broken by quantum computers — the ECDH key exchange, which Shor's algorithm 
         solves in polynomial time — while AES-256-GCM symmetric encryption, packet number
          protection, and Connection IDs all survive quantum attacks intact, confirming 
          that the migration is surgical and Harvest-Now-Decrypt-Later attacks on agent 
          sessions are operationally active today. On the performance side, a SLICES-RI benchmark 
          study demonstrated that ML-KEM adds less than 2 milliseconds of handshake overhead 
          under typical LAN conditions, that hybrid X25519+ML-KEM-768 is effectively as 
          fast as pure ECDH at NIST Level 1, and that the only meaningful bottleneck is 
          TCP's congestion window — a problem QUIC eliminates entirely. The 2025 Elsevier 
          comparative study then put QUIC and TCP/TLS head-to-head with post-quantum cryptography 
          enabled and found QUIC wins comprehensively: at 5% packet loss QUIC handshakes complete
           40–60% faster, QUIC's 0-RTT resumption makes the PQC handshake cost a one-time penalty for 
           returning agents, and QUIC's encrypted handshake prevents the middlebox interference that plagues
            large TLS exchanges over TCP. An MDPI survey of PQC adoption across the entire networking ecosystem 
            confirms that this migration is already underway globally — Chrome, Signal, Apple iMessage, AWS, and Cloudflare 
            have all deployed ML-KEM, the EU mandates PQC completion by 2035, and the US NSM-10 directive is already in force 
            — meaning A2A's migration aligns with rather than swims against industry momentum. The active IETF Internet-Draft on hybrid TLS 1.3 design provides
             the immediate deployment mechanism: X25519MLKEM768 combines classical ECDH and ML-KEM in a single handshake using a concatenation combiner, 
             so the session key depends on both secrets simultaneously — an attacker must break both to recover the key — and this is the exact scheme
 Chrome has used as its default for all HTTPS connections since April 2024, requiring zero protocol changes in A2A. However, the most urgent
  warning in this entire body of research comes from the KyberSlash paper, which won the IACR TCHES 2025 Best Paper Award: researchers discovered that
   Kyber's official C reference implementation contained a secret-dependent integer division instruction whose variable execution time leaked the complete 1184-byte secret key 
   in under 10 minutes on a Raspberry Pi, affecting Google Chrome, Signal, Mullvad VPN, and dozens of libraries before patches were issued — proving that mathematical soundness is 
   not enough and that A2A must mandate constant-time verified implementations, aggressive key-pair rotation, and diverse implementations across platforms. Finally, a comprehensive
    2025 arXiv survey of the full post-quantum cryptography landscape — covering lattice-based, hash-based, code-based, and isogeny-based schemes — delivers the strategic context: the
     isogeny-based SIKE was broken by a classical laptop in 77 minutes in 2022, confirming that PQC algorithm selection requires
 ongoing vigilance; HQC is being standardised as a non-lattice backup to ML-KEM for mathematical diversification; and Harvest-Now-Decrypt-Later is documented as confirmed active state-level
  adversary behaviour, making the quantum-secure upgrade of A2A's transport layer not a future consideration but an immediate operational necessity.
